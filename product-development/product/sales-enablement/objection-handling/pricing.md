# Objection: Pricing

> Stub — replace with your team's actual pricing objection-handling. **Last updated:** 2026-04-28.

## Common Forms

- "You're expensive"
- "Competitor X is cheaper"
- "We can build this internally"

## Response Frame: Anchor on Total Cost, Not Per-Seat

**Don't** defend the per-seat price. **Do** reframe to total cost of ownership.

| Customer Says | Our Response |
|---------------|-------------|
| "$X/seat is too much" | "What's it costing you today? If your team spends 5 hours/week on [our problem], at fully-loaded $150/hr that's $X/month per person. Our price is the markup on saving that time, not the time itself." |
| "Competitor X is half the price" | "Their model breaks at scale — they charge per feature, so when you add seat #50 you're suddenly paying $Y. See `battlecards/competitor-x.md` for the breakdown." |
| "We can build this internally" | "Build cost: ~$300K + 6 months of engineering time. Our annual is $Y. The buy/build break-even is ~14 months *if* your team builds it cleanly. Most don't." |

## Hard Cases

- **Procurement-driven**: get on a call with the procurement contact directly. Send `customers/case-studies/{customer}-procurement-approved.md` ahead of time.
- **Budget approved for less**: ask if they can move budget from another line item. If not, offer a smaller seat count (annual) at the budget they have.
- **Free trial expired without conversion**: usually means we didn't onboard them well — not a price problem. Schedule a usage review, not a pricing call.

## What Not to Say

- ❌ "We can probably do something on price" (signals you'll always discount)
- ❌ "Other customers pay more" (defensive)
- ❌ "It's worth it" (you have to prove it, not assert it)

## What to Say

- ✅ "Walk me through your current cost structure for this work" (reframe to TCO)
- ✅ "Let me share what [similar customer] achieved" (proof, not assertion)
- ✅ "I can offer annual at a 15% discount in exchange for a logo agreement" (concrete trade)
